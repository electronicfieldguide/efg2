Simple rules are:

  1. comma ',', means the same as 'or'
  2. whitespace before and after ',' is ignored
  3. '+', means the same as 'and'
  4. whitespace before and after '+' is ignored
  5. '+' means both /everything/ to the left and /everything/ to the right of the '+'. If there is more than one thing on either side, the rules are applied to each set of things as though independent of the '+' before combining them with the '+'.
  6. use parentheses to remove ambiguity. white space before and after parenthesis is ignored. Note that if you are describing a combination of things, like blue and yellow use paranthesis to distinguish them from the individual colors blue and yellow.
For instance in a description of shirts one could have (blue and white) and blue and white. Means that there is a blue and white shirt, a blue shirt, and a white shirt.
  1. (optional) For Media resources, captions that could be used on the media resource should be separated from the media resource name with a colon ':'.
Media Resources are things like image,audio,video etc. For example: Dorsal view: abc.jpg, Ventral view: zzz.jpg The application displays the Media resource with the caption 'Dorsal view'. The first Media resource in the list will be displayed on the search results page. In the above example for instance abc.jpg will be displayed on the search results page while both abc.jpg and zzz.jpg will be displayed on the taxon pages with the corresponding captions.

Implementation:

  1. alternatives are separated by comma's or the word 'or' 'blue, yellow, white' means 'blue or yellow or white'.
  2. last word in a phrase is the state, preceding word(s?) are modifiers. e.g blue, occasionally yellow blue, rarely yellow. 'occasionally' and 'rarely' are modifiers. [This one concerns me, I do not believe you can always assume which words are modifiers. In this example, what is "yellow" in the phrase "occasionally yellow blue"? Is it a modifier? What if I say something is "pale blue" - "pale" is a modifier in the grammatical sense, but do you really want the software to treat it as such? -- JenniferForman - 08 Nov 2005]
  3. 'ands' are supported with '+' or the word 'and'. For example 'blue and yellow' could be coded as 'blue + yellow.'
  4. ',', '+', 'or', 'and' are commutative 'blue + white' mean the same thing as 'white + blue' 'white,blue' means the same as 'blue,white' and so on.
  5. Standard boolean search precedence (some of which is counterintuitive in English, which has several inconsistent rules depending on the query answered by the boolean combination). Parentheses disambiguate. '+' takes precedence over ',', so the following are all the same. e.g: state is always blue and also either yellow or white: blue + yellow, white blue + (yellow,white) but to code that state is either blue or is yellow and white: blue, (yellow + white)

whereas blue, yellow and white blue or yellow and white (blue or yellow) and white all mean that the state is always white and also is either blue or yellow.

There is probably no simple English convention about and/or precedence. For example:

Q
What color shirts do you have:
A
"blue, yellow and white, green" usually means that some shirts are yellow and white. But "blue, green, yellow and white"

is probably dependent of the question and would for the shirt question mean "blue, green, yellow, or white", whereas "blue or yellow and white" probably means blue or (yellow and white)

A good tutorial is at http://www.health.library.mcgill.ca/eguides/boolean.htm

Example for point 5 above: Wing color: (blue or yellow) and (red or green) and (brown and white) could be coded as (blue,yellow)+(red,green)+(brown+white). The software could such for all taxon with wing color blue or yellow and intersect it with all taxon with wing color blue or green things and then intersect the results with taxon with wing color (brown and white) .

Maybe this is stating the obvious, but do you want to suggest to the authors that they drop the grammatical "and" in their lists if they are intending to truly convey "or"? e.g.: If you found a field of white, pink and purple flowers, you enter the data as:

"white, pink, purple" -- JenniferForman - 08 Nov 2005

This depends on whether they are describing the field or the flowers. Each flower is either white, pink, or purple. So if the rows are descriptions of fields of flowers (sensu ecology) and the character is colorOfFlowers, then the state is "white,pink,purple" If the character is colorOfField, then the state is "white and pink and purple".

This is a general problem about describing aggregates and composites. It is treated in Gregor Hagedorn's thesis, but not on the SDD wiki. Also, cf my shirt example above. Normal English has order dependencies in this case and those need disambiguation. -- BobMorris - 08 Nov 2005
